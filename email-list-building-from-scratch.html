How to Build an Email List
From Scratch
(The Right Way)

I want to tell you something that most online marketing content buries in paragraph fourteen, if it says it at all: your email list is the only audience you actually own.

Your Instagram following? Rented. Your TikTok channel? Rented. Your Facebook group? Definitely rented. Every one of those platforms can change its algorithm, suspend your account, or simply cease to exist — and the audience you spent years building goes with it.

Your email list is different. It lives in a file. You can export it, move it to a different platform, and email those people directly regardless of what any algorithm decides to do. That’s not a small thing. That’s the entire game.

“Building a social following without building an email list is like filling someone else’s bucket. The moment they take it away, you’re starting from zero.”

Here’s how to build one properly — from zero, without expensive tools, and without the mistakes I made when I was figuring this out.

What You Need Before You Start

Three things. That’s genuinely all.

1. An email marketing platform

This is the software that stores your subscribers, sends your emails, and handles unsubscribes automatically. You need one before you can collect a single address.

There are dozens of options. For beginners, the main contenders are:

  • GetResponse — solid free tier, excellent deliverability, built-in landing pages and automation. My recommendation for most people starting out.
  • MailerLite — clean interface, generous free plan, good automation on paid tiers.
  • ConvertKit (now Kit) — built specifically for creators and content publishers. Slightly higher learning curve, but powerful once you know it.
  • Mailchimp — everyone starts here because it’s the name they’ve heard. The free plan is restrictive and they’ve been hiking prices for years. I’d avoid it unless you have a specific reason to use it.
Don’t overthink the platform

Any of the above will work fine when you’re starting out. Pick one and move on. The platform doesn’t determine your results — what you do with it does. People have built six-figure businesses on free plans. The tool is not the bottleneck.

2. A lead magnet

A lead magnet is something valuable you give away in exchange for an email address. “Subscribe to my newsletter” is not a lead magnet. That’s just an ask with nothing on offer.

Good lead magnets solve a specific, immediate problem for a specific person. The best ones are things people would genuinely pay for — you’re giving them away to earn trust and an email address instead.

3. An opt-in form

The form where people enter their name and email. Your email platform handles this — you build the form inside the platform and embed it on your site. Most platforms also let you create standalone landing pages if you don’t have a website yet.

What Makes a Good Lead Magnet

This is where most people get it wrong. They create something generic — “10 Tips for Success” — and wonder why nobody subscribes.

A lead magnet works when it passes this test: would someone in my niche pay $10 for this if I was selling it? If the honest answer is no, it’s not specific or useful enough.

The formats that consistently convert well:

  • Checklists — quick to consume, immediately actionable. “The 7-step checklist for launching your first affiliate campaign” beats “Affiliate marketing tips.”
  • Short PDF guides — 5–15 pages of genuinely useful information on a narrow topic. Not a 50-page ebook nobody will finish.
  • Templates — email swipe files, spreadsheet trackers, content calendars. Things people can use immediately.
  • Mini video training — a single 10–20 minute video covering one specific skill. Works especially well if your audience watches video content.
  • Resource lists — curated tools, websites, or reading recommendations. The value is in the curation, not the length.

Where to Put Your Opt-In Form

Having a form is not enough — placement matters. Here’s where forms actually convert:

Dedicated landing page

A page with one job: get the sign-up. No navigation links to distract. No blog posts to wander off to. Just the lead magnet offer and the form. This is your highest-converting placement by a significant margin. Drive all your traffic here first.

Within blog posts

Inline forms placed after you’ve delivered value convert well because the reader is already engaged. A form halfway through a post that says “If you found this useful, you’ll want the free [lead magnet] I put together” is far more effective than a generic sidebar widget.

Exit intent pop-up

Triggers when a visitor moves their cursor toward the browser tab to leave. Not as annoying as it sounds when the offer is relevant — and it catches people who read your content but didn’t subscribe during the read.

Below every post

A simple sign-up block at the end of each article. People who read to the end are your warmest audience. Make it easy for them to stay connected.

What not to do

Don’t put a generic “Subscribe to my newsletter” form in the sidebar and call it done. Sidebar forms are largely ignored. Contextual forms — placed where they make sense relative to the content — convert five to ten times better.

How to Get Your First 100 Subscribers

The first hundred is the hardest. Not because the mechanics are difficult, but because you’re doing it without momentum, without proof, and usually without much traffic yet.

Here’s the honest path:

Tell people directly

Your first subscribers will almost always come from people you already know — not strangers on the internet. Tell your network. Post about it. Send a personal message to people who you think would find the lead magnet useful. This is not beneath you. Every list started this way.

Write content that ranks

SEO content is a slow burn, but it compounds. Find the questions people in your niche are actually searching for, write thorough answers, and put your opt-in form in every post. Three months from now, those posts will be delivering subscribers while you sleep.

Guest post or be a podcast guest

Find publications or podcasts that already reach your target audience and contribute. One well-placed guest post with a link back to your landing page can add dozens of highly qualified subscribers in a day.

Drive traffic to your landing page, not your homepage

When you’re promoting your content on social media or in communities, link to your dedicated opt-in landing page whenever it’s relevant. Your homepage is a general overview. Your landing page has one job.

What to Send Once People Subscribe

This is where most list builders fall apart. They spend months growing their list and then go silent — or send one email, get nervous about the responses, and disappear. By the time they come back, half their list has forgotten who they are.

Here’s the minimum viable email strategy:

Welcome email — send immediately

Deliver the lead magnet. Introduce yourself in two sentences. Tell them what to expect from your emails going forward. Keep it short. The welcome email has the highest open rate of anything you’ll ever send — use it to set the tone, not to pitch.

Nurture sequence — 3 to 5 emails over 1 to 2 weeks

After the welcome, send a short series of emails that build on the lead magnet. Each one should deliver value on its own — a useful tip, a common mistake, a short story with a lesson. Build trust before you ask for anything.

Regular broadcast emails

Once the sequence is done, email your list consistently. Weekly is the standard. Fortnightly if weekly feels like too much. The worst frequency is “whenever I remember” — that’s how you build a list that forgets you exist.

“An email list you don’t email is just a spreadsheet. Consistency is what turns subscribers into an audience.”

The Mistakes That Slow Everything Down

Waiting until you have a big audience to start

The time to start building your list is the moment you have any traffic at all — even ten visitors a day. A list started with ten subscribers today compounds into something real. A list you meant to start “once things pick up” is still at zero a year later.

A lead magnet that’s too broad

“The ultimate guide to digital marketing” attracts everyone and converts nobody. The more specific your lead magnet, the more specific your subscribers — and specific subscribers are worth ten times more than vague ones. You want people who have exactly the problem your offer solves.

Not emailing enough

Most beginners are terrified of emailing too often. In reality, the opposite problem is far more common: emailing so infrequently that subscribers forget they signed up. If someone is unsubscribing because you email too much, that’s feedback you can work with. Silence just breeds a cold, unresponsive list.

Treating the list like an ATM

Every email is a pitch. Every email is a promotion. No context, no relationship, just a constant stream of buy-this. This destroys open rates, kills trust, and turns a valuable asset into a liability. The ratio should always be more value than asks — a rough guide is three value emails for every one promotional email.

Buying or importing contacts

Buying a list is not building a list. Importing contacts who didn’t specifically sign up for your emails is not building a list. It’s violating anti-spam laws in most jurisdictions, and it produces terrible engagement that damages your sender reputation. Every subscriber on a quality list opted in. Every one.

One More Thing

There’s a version of this that sounds like a lot of work — and if you try to do everything at once, it is. But the actual starting point is simple: pick an email platform, create a single lead magnet, put up a landing page, and tell ten people about it.

That’s the whole first week. Everything else builds from there.

A year from now, the gap between people who started their list today and people who kept saying they would — that gap is enormous. And it’s not because the people who started were more talented or had more time. It’s because they started.
